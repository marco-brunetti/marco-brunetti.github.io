Cursed Silence: Demo (2023)

Youtube Gameplays

This was my first long-duration project. I decided to create a 15-minute vertical slice of a mystery horror game. With this project, I learned to overcome the struggles of long development cycles and an ever-growing code base. I presented Cursed Silence in public for the first time at a national game dev exhibition, and it garnered a good amount of attention. The concept was well received, but I noticed quite a few design flaws that confused players. I had designed the game in the most immersive way possible, with no UI, but during that exhibition, I learned that immersion means nothing if players don't know what to do next. In the following months, I improved my design choices, attempting to balance immersion and clarity. I launched the demo on itch.io and organically received dozens of YouTube gameplays, which provided valuable feedback that helped me improve my game development skills. I applied this newly absorbed knowledge to my next game, Cursed Silence: Christmas.

Breakthroughs:

  • Development cycle: I adapted my workflow to a long project, investing about 1200 hours.
  • Modularity: I learned how to create better and reusable code and developed many tools that could improve my workflow as a solo dev.
  • Storytelling: This was the first game that required active storytelling and visual elements.
  • Sound design: Although not perfect, it was a leap in quality compared to my previous projects.

Drawbacks:

  • Asset management: The number of assets in the scenes started to ramp up, and I had trouble maintaining a tidy project structure.
  • Burnout: This was a side project I developed for about 5 hours a day, in addition to my full-time day job. I wanted to break into the industry with a good project, but I had to improve my daily cycles afterwards.
